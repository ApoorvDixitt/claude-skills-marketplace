## 1. The Non-Negotiables

These rules apply to every X Article. No exceptions.

### Structure rules
- **Open with the content, not a preamble.** Never start with "In this article, I'll cover..." or "Let's dive in." Start with the thing itself.
- **Title: specific > clever.** "My Claude Code Setup Starts Every Session Already Knowing Everything" beats "The Future of AI Coding."
- **First 280 chars = your article's tweet.** What shows in the X feed preview card. Engineer it like a standalone post. If you can't stop the scroll with those chars, nobody opens the article.
- **Banner image is not optional.** A missing banner = a dead card in the feed. Always specify the visual concept.
- **Headers every 300–400 words.** Readers skim before they commit. Give them skim anchors.
- **Bold only the single most important sentence per section.** Over-bolding makes nothing feel important.
- **End with a specific reply-driving question.** Not "what do you think?" but "what's your CLAUDE.md look like?" or "what tool would you add?"

### Tone rules
- Write like you're talking to one specific person — a colleague who's smart but hasn't seen your exact approach
- Use "you" and "your" — conversational, not academic
- The moment writing becomes too "professional" it becomes boring. Write like you're talking to a good friend, not a lecture hall.
- Show the failure, not just the success. Readers trust writers who admit what didn't work.
- One opinion per section. Don't hedge everything. State a position.

## 2. Soul, Humanity & Voice — The Most Important Section

Removing AI patterns is only half the job. An article with no AI tells but also no voice is still obviously machine-made. It reads clean but dead.

### The core principle

X Articles that go viral feel like they were written by a specific person with a specific point of view who went through a specific experience. Not by a summarizing machine.

### Signs of soulless writing (even if technically "clean")

- Every sentence is the same length and structure
- No opinions, just neutral reporting
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings
- No first-person voice when it naturally belongs
- No humor, no edge, no personality
- Reads like a blog post optimized for search, not for a person

### How to inject voice — specific techniques

**Have an opinion in every section.** Don't just describe what something is — react to it. "I genuinely didn't expect this to make a difference" is more human than "this produced improved results."

**Vary rhythm deliberately.** Short punches land. Then a longer sentence that earns it by explaining why or adding context most writers skip. Then back to short.

**Acknowledge what didn't work.** Real humans ran into dead ends. "My first setup was wrong. Here's why" is more trustworthy than presenting only the final polished version.

**Be specific about feelings, not vague about emotions.** Not "this is useful" but "I stopped dreading new projects once this was in place."

**Use "I" with purpose.** First person isn't unprofessional — it's honest. "I still don't fully trust this" or "here's what surprised me" signals a real person thinking in real time.

**Leave some mess in.** Perfect structure feels algorithmic. A brief tangent, a self-interruption, a moment of "actually, let me rephrase that" — these are human.

**Write like you're talking to one specific person.** Not an audience. One colleague who's smart and hasn't seen your exact approach yet. That friend-to-friend specificity is irreplaceable.

### Before/after example

**Before (clean but soulless):**
> The session-start.sh script provides automatic context loading functionality. This results in improved efficiency and reduced onboarding time for each development session.

**After (has a pulse):**
> I got tired of spending the first ten minutes of every session re-explaining what I was building. session-start.sh fixed that. Now Claude walks in oriented. It's a small thing that changed how I actually want to start working.

### The final humanity pass

After completing the article, do this check before delivering:

1. Read the first sentence aloud. Would a real person say this?
2. Pick any two sections. Do they sound like the same person wrote them?
3. Find the single most specific sentence in the article. If you can't find one, the article isn't done.
4. Find the single most honest sentence — one that admits something uncomfortable or uncertain. If you can't find one, add one.
5. Ask: "If this writer's name was removed, could you identify their voice from this alone?" That's the standard.

## 3. Anti-AI Pattern Checklist (Articles Edition)

Before finalizing any article, check for these patterns. They signal AI generation and kill trust.

### Critical (always fix)

**Significance inflation** — Making ordinary things sound profound.
- Bad: "This fundamentally transforms the paradigm of how we approach development."
- Good: "This means Claude starts sharp instead of starting from scratch."

**AI vocabulary words** — Never use these: actually, additionally, delve, pivotal, tapestry, landscape (abstract), testament, underscore (verb), showcase, vibrant, foster, garnered, highlight (verb), leverage (as a verb), navigate (abstract), streamline, robust, realm, journey (abstract), revolutionize, game-changing.

**Em dash overuse** — One per article max. Use commas or short sentences instead.

**Vague attributions** — "Research shows", "Studies suggest", "Experts say" with no source.
- Bad: "Research shows that longer context windows improve performance."
- Good: "code-review-graph cuts token usage 6.8x on code reviews (tested across 6 real repos)."

**Rule of three** — The compulsive need to list exactly three things, three qualities, three examples. Vary it.

**Sycophantic framing** — "Great question!", "This is a fascinating topic", "I'm excited to share..."

**Copula avoidance** — Rewriting "X is Y" as "X represents", "X constitutes", "X encompasses". Just use "is".

**Passive voice hiding agency** — "Mistakes were made" → "I made mistakes." Own it.

### Common (fix where found)

**Promotional language** — "Cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "world-class", "best-in-class". These mean nothing. Describe what the thing actually does.

**Inflated symbolism** — Over-metaphorizing simple concepts. "The CLAUDE.md is the beating heart of the entire system, pulsing with intelligence..." → "CLAUDE.md is where behavior is defined."

**Superficial -ing analyses** — "By implementing this system, we are fundamentally reimagining what AI assistance can be." → Just show what it does.

**The "tapestry" problem** — Describing things as woven together, interwoven, interconnected, symbiotic. These are content-free filler words.

**Formulaic transitions** — "With that said...", "Moving on...", "Now that we've covered X, let's explore Y..."

**Excessive hedging** — "It's worth noting that", "It's important to mention", "It should be pointed out". Cut and state directly.

**Noun pile-ups** — "Implementation optimization strategy framework" — just use short plain nouns.

### Section-level checks

- Does every section have exactly one main point?
- Does every header work as a standalone sentence?
- Is there at least one concrete specific (number, name, tool, command) per section?
- Is there any section that could be deleted without losing anything essential?
- Does the closing question require knowledge of the article to answer?

## 4. Voice Calibration

### Default voice (when no sample is provided)
- Direct, first-person, opinionated, specific
- Short punches. Longer sentences to earn them.
- No fluff. Real details over vague claims.
- Confident but not arrogant — "Here's what I found" not "Here's the definitive truth"
- Tech/builder audience: can assume familiarity with code, tools, GitHub, CLIs
- Write like a smart friend explaining something over Slack, not a blog post optimized for SEO

### Calibrating to user voice
If the user provides a writing sample:
- Analyze sentence length patterns (punchy vs. flowing?)
- Note word choice register (casual vs. precise?)
- Identify recurring phrases or verbal tics
- Check how they handle uncertainty and opinions

Match that voice throughout — don't just remove AI patterns, replace them with *their* patterns.

### Personal brand voice anchors for Apoorv
Based on existing writing samples:
- Deeply human, emotionally grounded
- Natural phrasing, not corporate
- First-person, builds from personal experience outward
- No jargon inflation — prefers plain language with technical precision where needed
- Builder identity — the writing comes from someone who builds, not just someone who observes

## 5. Companion Tweet Algorithm Rules

The companion tweet is the article's entry point. It goes in the X feed; the article doesn't.

### Algorithm facts (current as of 2025–2026)
- **Links in tweet body reduce reach by ~1,700%** — put the article link at the very end of the tweet, or in the first reply
- **First 30–60 minutes matter most** — reply to every comment in this window to trigger the algorithm's engagement velocity signal
- **Bookmarks > likes** in algorithmic weight — write tweets that people want to save
- **Replies > reposts** — end with a specific question that generates easy one-line responses
- **Max 1–2 hashtags** — never generic ones; 3+ = 40% reach penalty
- **No bold in tweet body** — looks like LinkedIn
- **No curly quotes** — use straight quotes

### Companion tweet structure options

**Option A — The Insight Lead (best for technical/system articles):**
```
[One-sentence core insight from the article]

[2–3 lines of specific proof or context]

[Link]

[Question]
```

**Option B — The Story Lead (best for personal/journey articles):**
```
[Specific moment or state from the before]

[The shift — one sentence]

[What changed as a result — specific]

[Link]
```

**Option C — The Claim Lead (best for contrarian or conviction articles):**
```
[Bold claim — standalone, doesn't need context]

[Why this is true — 1–2 lines, specific]

[Link]

[Optional: "Agree?"]
```

### What NOT to do in companion tweets
- "I just published an article about..." — passive, no hook
- Summarizing the article in the tweet (no reason to click)
- Asking people to "check out" the article
- Multiple questions at the end
- Starting with a question (weaker engagement signal than a statement)

## 6. Posting & Promotion Strategy

### Timing
- **Best posting windows**: Tuesday–Thursday, 9–11 AM local time (for B2B/tech audience)
- Post when you can be present for the first 30–60 minutes to reply to comments
- Avoid Friday afternoons and weekends for long-form content (lower intent engagement)

### Immediately after posting
1. Reply to your own companion tweet with the direct article link (keeps reach on the tweet while making link accessible)
2. Reply to the first few comments within 10 minutes — signals active engagement to the algorithm
3. If you have a second account or collaborators, a retweet or quote-tweet in the first hour amplifies distribution

### First 24 hours
- Quote-tweet your article companion tweet with a different angle or excerpt
- Drop a pull quote from the article as a standalone tweet, linking back
- Reply to related conversations in your niche with "wrote about this here" + link

### Promoting the article body
- Embed key sections or data points as images in follow-up posts
- If the article has a table or diagram, post it as an image with a link to the full piece
- Pull the most quotable line from the article and post it as a standalone tweet

### Repurposing
- LinkedIn: paste a condensed version of the article with a link to the full X Article
- Newsletter: excerpt + link back
- GitHub README (for technical articles): link the X Article as a related resource

## 7. Subscriber-Only Article Tips

If using X's subscriber paywall for premium articles:

### Free vs. paid split strategy
- Make introductory or "how I approach X" articles free — they build audience and signal value
- Reserve deep-dive systems, detailed breakdowns, and exclusive setups for subscribers
- Use the free article's companion tweet as promotional material for the subscriber version

### Teaser tactics
- Publish a free article that covers 20% of a topic, then link to the paid deep-dive
- End free articles with "subscriber version covers: [3 specific things]" — concrete, not vague
- Occasional callouts in subscriber articles: "X people now get this in their inbox" — social proof

### Retention mechanics
- End each subscriber article with "Next: [specific thing coming next]" — creates expectation
- Create a recurring series (weekly memo, monthly breakdown) — consistency builds habit
- Reference past articles in new ones — rewards loyal readers and creates content depth

### Promoting subscriber articles
- Post teaser companion tweet normally to full audience
- The locked content stays visible in the feed with a Subscribe button embedded
- Link to subscription paywall: `https://x.com/YOURUSERNAME/creator-subscriptions/subscribe`
