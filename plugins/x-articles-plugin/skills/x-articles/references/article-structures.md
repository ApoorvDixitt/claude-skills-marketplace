## 1. Framework Selection Guide

| If the article is about... | Use this framework |
|---|---|
| A personal system, workflow, or setup | Behind-the-Machine |
| A journey from bad → good state | Arc of Change |
| Step-by-step how-to / technical breakdown | The Definitive Breakdown |
| An opinion that goes against conventional wisdom | The Conviction Piece |
| A hard experience or failure with lessons | The War Story |
| Tools, resources, or curated knowledge | The Arsenal |

## 2. The Six Frameworks

### Framework 1: Behind-the-Machine
*Best for: "how I really do X", personal systems, setups, workflows*
*Voice: insider sharing a secret that actually works*

**Section order:**
1. **The problem** — What it was like before. Specific, not vague. One painful moment or pattern.
2. **The naive fix** — What most people do (and why it fails). Brief.
3. **The system** — Your actual setup, piece by piece. This is the heart of the article. Use numbered sections or H2s for each component.
4. **The net effect** — What changed. Concrete outcomes, not feelings.
5. **The edge** — The one thing that makes this setup better than alternatives. Bold claim.
6. **CTA** — What the reader can do with this right now. Question or next step.

**Opening hook formula:**
> "Most [people/devs/founders] [do obvious thing]. I made it so [unexpected better outcome]. Here's the full setup."

**Example title formulas:**
- "My [X] Setup That [Surprising Result]. Here's Every Piece."
- "How I Made [Tool] [Do Surprising Thing] Before I [Normal Action]."
- "The [System] That Changed How I [Core Activity]. Full Breakdown."

---

### Framework 2: Arc of Change
*Best for: before/after transformations, founder journeys, personal growth*
*Voice: honest narrator who went through something real*

**Section order:**
1. **The before** — Where you started. Be specific. A date, a number, a failure.
2. **The break** — The moment that forced change. Concrete event, not a gradual realization.
3. **The experiment** — What you tried. Failures included.
4. **The shift** — What actually worked. The non-obvious thing.
5. **The after** — Where you are now. Numbers or outcomes.
6. **The lesson** — What this means for the reader. Generalizable.
7. **CTA** — What's the reader's "before" right now?

**Opening hook formula:**
> "[X time ago]: [bad specific state]. Today: [good specific state]. The only thing that changed:"

**Example title formulas:**
- "[X] Months Ago I Was [Bad State]. Here's What Changed."
- "The One Thing That [Transformed Result] in [Timeframe]."
- "I Used to [Common Bad Practice]. Then I Found [System/Insight]. Here's What Happened."

---

### Framework 3: The Definitive Breakdown
*Best for: tutorials, technical how-tos, "everything you need to know about X"*
*Voice: expert removing confusion, making the complex simple*

**Section order:**
1. **Why this matters** — The stakes. Why should the reader care right now?
2. **The core concept** — Define the key idea in one paragraph. No jargon.
3. **Step-by-step / component-by-component** — The actual breakdown. H2 for each step or component. Include code, screenshots, tables where relevant.
4. **The gotchas** — What trips people up. Honest.
5. **The quick-start version** — TL;DR for people who want to implement right now.
6. **CTA** — Question about their experience or a link to the next step.

**Opening hook formula:**
> "Most people get [topic] wrong because [specific misconception]. Here's what's actually going on — and how to fix it."

**Example title formulas:**
- "The Complete [Topic] Breakdown (That Nobody Wrote Yet)."
- "[Number] Things You Need to Know About [Topic] Before You [Action]."
- "Everything Wrong With How People Use [Tool] — and What to Do Instead."

---

### Framework 4: The Conviction Piece
*Best for: contrarian opinions, takes that go against popular advice, debates*
*Voice: confident, evidence-backed, not just provocative for its own sake*

**Section order:**
1. **The consensus** — What everyone believes. State it fairly.
2. **The crack** — Where the consensus starts to break down. Specific evidence or observation.
3. **The conviction** — Your actual position. Bold and clear.
4. **The evidence** — Why you believe this. Data, examples, logic. Don't skimp.
5. **The objection** — What critics will say. Address it directly.
6. **The implication** — What this means if you're right. What should the reader do differently?
7. **CTA** — "Where do you land on this?" or "Here's what I'm watching to see if I'm right."

**Opening hook formula:**
> "Everyone says [conventional wisdom]. They're not wrong — but they're missing the part that actually matters. Here's what I mean."

**Example title formulas:**
- "The [Common Advice] Everyone Gives Is Wrong. Here's What Actually Works."
- "I've Been [Common Practice] for [X Time]. Here's Why I'm Stopping."
- "[Popular Tool/Approach] Is Overrated. Here's What I Use Instead (and Why)."

---

### Framework 5: The War Story
*Best for: hard experiences, failures, near-misses, pivotal decisions*
*Voice: honest, slightly raw, reflective — not self-pitying*

**Section order:**
1. **The drop-in** — Start in the middle of the crisis or moment. No setup.
2. **The background** — Quick context. One paragraph max.
3. **The escalation** — How it got worse. Specific.
4. **The decision point** — The moment where something had to change.
5. **The outcome** — What happened. Don't oversell the resolution.
6. **The lesson** — What you actually learned. Make it transferable.
7. **CTA** — "What would you have done?" or a question about the reader's version of this.

**Opening hook formula:**
> "[Specific bad moment]. That was [X time ago]. Here's what I learned."

**Example title formulas:**
- "The [Decision/Mistake] That Cost Me [Concrete Loss] — and What I'd Do Differently."
- "I Almost [Failed at Important Thing]. Here's the Exact Moment It Changed."
- "The Worst [Time Period] of My [Professional Life] — and What Came After."

---

### Framework 6: The Arsenal
*Best for: curated tools, resources, bookmarks, "what I use" content*
*Voice: expert curator — not listing for the sake of listing, but explaining why each item earns its place*

**Section order:**
1. **The criteria** — What makes something worth including. Sets your standards.
2. **The items** — One H2 per item. For each: what it is (1 sentence), why it's on this list (2–3 sentences), the link.
3. **The stack in context** — How these things work together. Optional but powerful.
4. **The honorable mentions** — Things that almost made it but didn't. Builds credibility.
5. **CTA** — "What would you add?" or "What am I missing?"

**Opening hook formula:**
> "I've spent [X time/money] testing [category]. These are the [N] things that actually survived."

**Example title formulas:**
- "The [N] [Tools/Resources/Systems] I Actually Use for [Purpose]."
- "Everything in My [Purpose] Stack — and Why Each Thing Earned Its Place."
- "[N] Things I Use Every Day That Most [Audience] Have Never Heard Of."

## 3. Section Header Formulas

Headers in X Articles serve two jobs: navigation for readers who skim, and mini-hooks that make someone want to read the section. Every header should work as a standalone sentence.

**Strong header patterns:**
- State the mechanism: "session-start.sh: The Gate"
- Lead with the outcome: "The Knowledge Graph (6.8x Fewer Tokens)"
- Use tension: "The Part That Actually Matters"
- Number with specificity: "3 Rules That Apply Everywhere, No Exceptions"
- Make a claim: "Auto-Bootstrap: New Projects Set Themselves Up"

**Weak header patterns (avoid):**
- Generic nouns: "Introduction", "Overview", "Conclusion"
- Vague: "Background", "Context", "More Information"
- Complete sentences that close a loop: use headers to open loops, not close them

## 4. Virality Patterns Specific to X Articles

Unlike threads, Articles don't go viral inside the feed on their own — they go viral because the **companion tweet** goes viral, then people click through. The article itself builds authority and dwell time; the tweet builds reach.

**What makes an Article get widely shared:**

1. **The "save for later" article**: Dense, genuinely useful, reference-material quality. People bookmark these and share them as "the best thing I've read on [topic]."

2. **The "this is exactly what I needed" article**: Solves a specific problem that many people have but few have written about cleanly.

3. **The "I didn't know this was possible" article**: Reveals a non-obvious approach, tool, or system. Creates genuine surprise.

4. **The "same thing, way better" article**: Takes a known topic and covers it with significantly more depth, honesty, or specificity than anything else that exists.

**Platform-specific virality signals:**
- X's algorithm rewards dwell time — longer articles that keep people reading get more algorithmic push
- Articles that generate replies get extra boost (dwell time signal from comments)
- Articles shared by multiple accounts in a short window get amplified in the For You feed
- Embedding relevant tweets inside the article adds interactivity and can trigger notifications to those users

**What kills X Article virality:**
- Generic title ("My Thoughts on Claude Code")
- Weak first 280 chars (nobody clicks through)
- No banner image (card looks dead in the feed)
- Too polished / corporate tone (doesn't feel like a real person)
- Listicle padding (10 items where 4 were actually useful)

## 5. Formatting Guide for X Articles Editor

X Articles editor (accessed via Articles tab → Write) supports:

**Text formatting:**
- **Bold** (Ctrl+B / Cmd+B) — use for the one most important sentence per section
- *Italic* (Ctrl+I / Cmd+I) — use for emphasis within a sentence, never whole paragraphs
- ~~Strikethrough~~ — use sparingly for effect
- Indentation — use for blockquotes or nested points

**Structure elements:**
- H1 (Heading) — only use for major section breaks (2–3 per article max)
- H2 (Subheading) — use for sub-sections within major sections
- Body — default text
- Numbered lists — use for steps, sequences, ranked items
- Bullet lists — use for parallel items with no sequence
- Blockquote — use for pull quotes, key callouts, or embedded quotes

**Media:**
- Images — insert inline; caption is optional but helps SEO/discoverability
- GIFs — use for demonstrating UI flows or short clips
- Embedded X posts — insert by pasting post URL; appears as rich card
- Video — native upload or embed
- Links — inline text links preferred over bare URLs

**Table formatting:**
- X Articles renders markdown-style tables cleanly
- Use tables for comparison, tool stacks, or structured data
- Keep tables narrow (3 columns max for mobile readability)

**Mobile considerations:**
- Most X users read on mobile — write for narrow screens
- Short paragraphs (2–3 sentences) over long dense blocks
- Headers every 300–400 words
- Images break up scroll fatigue

**Banner image specs:**
- Recommended: 1200×628px (5:2 aspect ratio)
- File types: JPG, PNG, GIF
- Keep critical content away from edges (may be cropped on some displays)
- High contrast works best — this is a thumbnail-sized card in the feed
